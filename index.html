Ancient Roots:

Science’s earliest roots can be traced back to Ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia around 3000 to 1200 BCE. These civilizations made significant contributions to mathematics, astronomy, and medicine, which later influenced Greek natural philosophy. Greek philosophers attempted to explain natural events based on natural causes.

Middle Ages and Renaissance:

After the fall of the Western Roman Empire, knowledge of Greek science declined in Latin-speaking Western Europe. However, Greek works were preserved and absorbed in the Byzantine Empire and the Arabic-speaking Muslim world. Translations of Greek texts reintroduced natural philosophy to Western Europe.

Scientific Revolution:

The 16th-17th centuries marked the Scientific Revolution. New ideas and discoveries departed from previous Greek conceptions. The emergence of a more mechanistic worldview, integration with mathematics, and a defined scientific method transformed natural philosophy into modern science.

Subsequent Revolutions:

The 18th-century chemical revolution introduced quantitative methods for chemistry. The 19th century saw perspectives on energy conservation, Earth’s age, and evolution come into focus1. In summary, the history of science reflects humanity’s journey from observing natural phenomena to achieving modern scientific understanding.
